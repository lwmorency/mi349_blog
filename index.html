Designing a Morning Routine That Actually Sticks

By Luke Morency · September 27, 2025

If you’ve ever declared, “Tomorrow I’ll wake up early and change everything,” you’re not alone. The real trick to a morning routine that actually survives the first week isn’t motivation; it’s designing friction-light steps you can repeat on autopilot. I like to think of routines as UX for your day. Instead of a heroic hour-long ritual, start with three tiny actions that take less than five minutes total: drink a glass of water, put your phone face-down in another room, and write one line about what would make today a win. That’s it. When those become effortless, expand them. Pair habits using “after I ___, I will ___.” After I brush my teeth, I’ll open the curtains. After I open the curtains, I’ll do ten slow breaths by the window. In the background, control the inputs: prep your outfit at night, set a single gentle alarm, and keep your desk clear so the first thing you see invites focus. The goal isn’t a perfect morning; it’s removing decisions so momentum carries you forward. Over time, you’ll notice the compounding effect—less scramble, more intention, and a day that starts with a small win rather than a sprint.

To make the routine tangible, try tracking your first week with a simple table. Don’t aim for flawless streaks; aim for noticing patterns. If Wednesday always collapses, plan a shorter routine that day and declare it a success. Progress looks like consistency that survives chaos.

Beginner Strength: The Three-by-Five Blueprint

By Luke Morency · September 20, 2025

Starting a strength program can feel like opening a toolbox you’ve never used—lots of shiny options, no idea where to begin. The three-by-five blueprint keeps things simple: choose three compound lifts (squat, press, pull), train them three days per week, and do five heavy reps for three sets each. Compound movements recruit the most muscle per minute and teach coordination you’ll use in daily life: standing up with groceries, bracing your core, or hoisting a suitcase overhead. Focus on slow progress, not heroics. Add 2.5–5 lbs per session as long as your form stays crisp. Warm up with lighter sets, rest two to three minutes between work sets, and stop one rep shy of failure. If a session feels rough, keep the weight the same next time and prioritize sleep and protein. Speaking of which, your kitchen is part of the program: aim for a gram of protein per pound of goal bodyweight across real meals, not just shakes. Track workouts with pen and paper so progress is visible. Finally, give yourself a clear exit: the workout ends when the last set ends—no random burnout circuits. You’ll leave energy to recover and arrive at the next session ready to build again. Eight weeks from now, your logbook will tell the story: steady numbers, better posture, and a confidence that outlifts the noise.
